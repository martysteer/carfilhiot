Typical Event Formats

Individual Sprint (sometimes called Grand Prix)

Each driver gets four or five heats, starting in a different grid position each time. The heats have up to six karts in them and are mixed up by an electric computer, or by a chap with a piece of paper and a crayon, so that groups of friends who checked in together don't necessarily race together. Everyone who finishes a given heat is awarded points, sometimes even the non-finishers, but the nearer the front you finish, the more points you get. At the end of the heats, the points are added up and used to award grid positions in the semi-finals. Sometimes, everyone gets a semi-final, but usually it's about 60% of the entries. The first two (or three) in each semi get a place on the grid of the final. As always, points and places can be deducted for unfair tactics like ramming, switching off competitors' engines and ignoring marshals' flag signals.

Team Enduro (sometimes called Le Mans)

In theory, enduro teams are a specific size, usually four to six, but experience has shown that it is hard to get everyone to turn up, and the rule is often bent to specify a minimum number of driver changes, so that teams are frequently as small as two.

In an enduro, each team drives as a relay, changing drivers when they see fit, until the time is up, and the team with the most laps wins. Enduro lengths are anything from two to twenty-four hours.

From a practical point of view, most people lose their edge after half an hour and some are exhausted after fifteen minutes. I have done a one-hour, one-driver enduro, and though I could still drive at the end of the hour, I found some difficulty in walking afterwards!

Driver changes, however, are time-consuming, and you have to balance driver efficiency against time wasted in changing.

Initial grid positions may be determined by lot, or by choosing an arbitrary moment at which to start the race with all the teams rolling, or by lap times achieved in a practice session. Aldershot even uses a Le Mans start with drivers running to their karts.

Team Sprint

This format is less common than the other two. As in individual sprint, each driver gets four or five heats, starting in a different grid position each time. The heats have up to six karts in them. During the heats, only one driver from any given team will appear in any given heat.

    The second half of the meeting usually takes one of the following forms:

  • In one form, the second half is a mini enduro using the current points standings to determine initial grid position.
  • In another, the team is invited to choose which team member will participate in each final (there is a different final for each team member, but the grid positions for the team are different in each case). The points for the finals are added to those of the heats for each team.
    Sometimes, in this format, double points are awarded for places gained, and single points deducted for places lost, rather than points being awarded for finishing position, so that starting a good driver from your team at the back of the grid becomes a good idea, and adds to the fun.
